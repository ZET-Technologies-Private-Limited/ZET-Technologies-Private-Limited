The Observation

A rainy Friday in Bangalore changed everything

In 2022, I was leading logistics logistics at Swiggy. On a Friday evening during a heavy monsoon, I watched our ops dashboard go red ‚Äî orders piling up, workers idle, and customers cancelling.

But what struck me wasn't the chaos inside our platform. It was what I saw outside: an Amazon delivery agent sitting under an awning 600 metres from our fulfilment centre, playing on his phone. Their platform was quiet. Ours was on fire.

Two companies. One city. Dozens of trained workers ‚Äî - no mechanism to share them where demand was highest. That seemed profoundly, avoidably broken.

‚Äî Kranthi Kiran, reflecting on the founding insight

I spent the next three months interviewing 200 gig workers across Bangalore, Hyderabad, and Chennai. The pattern was identical in every city: workers wanted multi-platform income. Platforms wanted flexible capacity. Nobody had built the middleware to connect them.

The Problem

Why nobody had solved this yet

The reason this problem existed wasn't technical. It was structural. Platforms built their workforce vertically ‚Äî recruit, train, deploy, pay ‚Äî because treating workers as proprietary assets felt like a competitive moat.

But this moat came at enormous cost: idle workers during off-peak hours, capacity crises during surges, and gig workers trapped on a single app's earnings ceiling.

I realised the real moat wasn't the workers ‚Äî it was data, relationships, and network effects. A platform that facilitates the shared economy of labour would actually attract more partners, more workers, and more data than any closed system.

The analogy I kept coming back to was packet routing on the internet. Nobody owns a packet in transit ‚Äî it flows to wherever it's needed. Why couldn't a trained delivery worker flow the same way?

The Decision

Leaving a comfortable senior role to start from zero

I left Swiggy in January 2023. My co-founder Venkata Sadwil, who I'd worked with briefly at Ola, joined two months later. We had no funding, no office, and a prototype built on our laptops in my apartment in Koramangala.

The first 50 workers we onboarded were recruited in person at metro stations and marketplaces. We offered them ‚Çπ500 per day guaranteed while we tested the allocation system ‚Äî and many of them are still with us today.

We ran 90 days of real operations before we touched a single investor conversation. We wanted proof first. By the end of those 90 days, we had 50 workers, 2 platform partners, and our average allocation time was 8 seconds.

The Company

What Zet is ‚Äî and what it deliberately isn't

Zet Technologies is not trying to become the next Swiggy or Zomato. We are not a consumer-facing delivery brand. We have no ambitions of owning the last mile.

We are building the infrastructure layer that powers everyone else. Like AWS for compute, or Stripe for payments ‚Äî we want Zet to be the default workforce layer that every service platform in India plugs into.

Our north star metric is simple: when a platform joins Zet, does the average gig worker on our network earn more? Every product decision, every engineering investment, every partnership is evaluated against that question first.

Building Philosophy

How Arjun thinks about building companies

01
Infrastructure before product
The most valuable companies are often invisible. They're the rails underneath the consumer-facing stuff. Build the foundation, not the house ‚Äî and build it to hold 100 houses.
02
Operate before you automate
We ran manual operations for 90 days before writing a line of automation code. You cannot automate what you don't fully understand. Understanding comes from doing, not designing.
03
Worker economics are a product metric
If a gig worker doesn't earn more by joining Zet, the product has failed. Not the worker. Not the market. The product. This keeps us honest about what we're actually optimising for.
04
Small team, high trust, no committees
Every person at Zet can make a meaningful decision independently. Hiring the the 20 people beats hiring 200 adequate ones. Trust is infrastructure too ‚Äî and it's harder to build than code.
